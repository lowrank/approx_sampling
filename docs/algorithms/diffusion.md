# Diffusion Sampling

## How It Works

A score network is trained via denoising score matching to estimate the gradient of the log-density of an error-weighted target distribution. Samples are generated by annealed Langevin dynamics: starting from uniform noise, points are iteratively updated by following the score with decreasing noise levels. Reflecting boundary conditions keep samples within the domain. The noise schedule is annealed from high to low variance across sampling steps.

## Pros

- Score-based models can represent arbitrarily complex, multi-modal distributions.
- Annealed Langevin dynamics mixes well and avoids local modes.
- Denoising score matching provides a stable, scalable training objective.
- No adversarial training — avoids min-max instability.

## Cons

- Langevin sampling requires many iterative steps, making it slow at inference.
- Reflecting boundaries add implementation complexity.
- Score network may be poorly estimated in low-density regions.
- Noise schedule and step size are critical hyperparameters requiring tuning.

## Reference
